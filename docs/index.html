Primary nominee model vs Polymarket candidate markets — UPCOMING primaries only (the inverse of the Model vs Markets tab, which needs primaries decided). Model = XGBoost nominee probability trained on historical contested partisan primaries (polls + fundraising + field structure), normalized within each race. Market = Polymarket quote, vig-normalized across the race book when the listed candidates sum to ≥85%. Edge = model − market: blue right = model likes the candidate more than the market. Primary polling is structurally less accurate than general-election polling (late deciders, name recognition) — treat small edges as noise, and thin poll counts (yellow) with extra suspicion. Jungle/top-two states (CA, WA, LA, AK) are out of scope.

Same race, different prices across prediction markets. Raw gap = difference in implied probability between platforms (Dem win probability for general-election rows; nominee probability for the named candidate on primary-candidate rows). Net gap subtracts estimated round-trip fees (Kalshi ~2%, Polymarket ~2%, PredictIt ~12%). Positive net gap = theoretical arb. ★ Guaranteed means a Buy-YES + Buy-NO basket across the two platforms costs <$1 after fees at real fillable quotes (live ask / NO-ask, not midpoints) — locked profit either way the race resolves. Settles is when both legs pay out; the green %/yr is the guaranteed return annualized over that lock-up (sort by it to find arbs that cash soonest). Tradeable shows max contracts you could buy on each side (A / B) before paying ≥3pp worse than best ask — the smaller side caps the realistic trade. Red <50, yellow <500, green ≥500.

Glossary

Who can vote (Primary type)

Closed. Only voters registered with that party can vote in its primary. Independents and other-party voters are locked out.

Semi-closed. Registered party members vote in their party's primary, but unaffiliated/independent voters can also choose one. Members of the opposing party are still locked out.

Open. Anyone can vote in either party's primary regardless of registration. You typically pick on the day.

Top-two (jungle). No separate party primaries. All candidates from all parties run on the same ballot, and the top two vote-getters advance to the general regardless of party (CA, LA, WA).

Majority runoff. Standard partisan primary, but if no candidate hits >50% the top two go to a runoff election a few weeks later (GA, AL, AR, MS, OK, SC, TX, some NC races).

Top-4 + RCV (AK). Top-four nonpartisan jungle primary, then the November general uses ranked-choice voting.

How votes are counted (Method)

FPTP — First-Past-The-Post. Plurality wins. One round of voting, the candidate with the most votes wins, no majority required. Default in most states.

Runoff. Counts can go to a second round. Either a true majority runoff (must hit >50%) or a top-two jungle that proceeds to a head-to-head.

RCV — Ranked-Choice Voting. Voters rank candidates. Lowest-ranked are eliminated and their votes redistributed by next preference until someone has a majority. Used at the federal level in Alaska.
